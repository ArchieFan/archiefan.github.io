1.What is data quality?

 

Refers to accessing your subject data to make sure its fit for your intended purpose.  Dimensions/aspects includes accuracy, completeness, reliability, consistency, formatting, freshness, etc. Data quality is about ensuring that we have data of good quality and the data is fit for purpose.

 

 

 

 

 

1.1Accuracy - Accuracy is about the current state of your data  versus reality. 

1.2Completeness -  is about how you have filled out all the required fields in your dataset.

1.3Consistency -  is about how uniform your data set is throughout different data sources. Data from different data source must end up with same format.

1.4Uniqueness - is largely tied to  the number of duplicates I have in a dataset.  

 

When poor data creeps into your systems, it can affect credibility.  We need our audience to be thinking about what our data means not questioning its validity.  And if poor data does get in it can be expensive to repair both the reputation of your data and in some cases actual damage to the repository.

 

⚫Effects usability, trustworthiness, reliability of datasets

⚫can be costly t remediate bad data

11.Good response to bad data

 

11.1Data in Motion

 

 

 

most data quality issues arise when you put your data in motion. There are of course times where static data can end up getting poor data quality but in the most part it's the movement of data whether it's an integration or you're ingesting new data into your system or reshaping your data.

For use in some warehouse, it's the movement where the data is at higher risk and that movement typically involves a data pipeline with multiple stages each of those stages capable of introducing or allowing poor data to creep in.

 

 

 

Three actions that you can take when you design your data pipelines to keep poor data quality in mind.

1)Validation and Profiling

2)Look at data quality in each pipeline stage

3)Manage response to data errors

 

 

 

 

11.2 Validation and Profiling

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The most obvious response to bad data to prepare for bad data is to add stages to your data pipeline that explicitly deal with data quality. They can go anywhere in the pipe but minimally think about adding bookends at the front and the back of your data pipe to scrutinize your data on the way in and on the way out.  These kinds of explicit data quality checks in two different categories validation and profiling.

 

11.2.1 Validation

 

The most common type of data quality check where you apply business rules to each record at a particular stage.

 

 

 

 

 

⚫Does an id field have the right number of characters in the correct expected pattern ?

⚫Is the begin timestamp less than the end time stamping up in this quarter?

⚫There are three phone number fields, Do i have at least one that has a valid phone number?

11.2.2 Profiling

 

Data quality check unlike validation where we're looking at each record one by one and applying business rules profiling is generic and independent of any business rule.  it takes an entire data set and creates a statistical profile that can be used to detect questionable data and prevent it from entering the system or moving through your pipeline. (Cross-check the data)

So we want to try to capture bad data as close to the cause as we can to allow efficient resolution. If we don't make that attempt to capture those issues as soon as they arise, but instead allow them to propagate downstream into multiple stages, further stages of our pipeline, it can become much more time consuming to track back and figure out where exactly the data quality issue initially arose.  writing unit tests for each method that they create.

 

 

 

 

11.3Manage response to data errors

 

Managing risk is to actually consider managing responses to your data errors as opposed to just capturing them.

 

 

 

 

 

 

11.3.1 Data error management

 

 

 

I.T staff is maybe typically the first person to detect or become aware that there are errors or data quality issues in the data. But  I.T team is probably not the best choice for actually making the corrections. They may not have the right business context to know how to correct a particular set of data issues or they may not have the authority to make those changes even if they do know what the values are. But more deeply than that, having I.T in charge of making these corrections can be a missed opportunity to correct the issue at its source and prevent I.T from having to correct it again. So best to involve business users or domain experts in the data error manager process as someone who knows how and when to correct particular data quality issues.

 

 

11.3.2 Data about your bad data

 

The last point about data error management is that each of these stages that's reporting into the data error manager should use a standard format for how it reports that error. 

 

we don't want the data error manager to have to deal with those formats. we want the data error manager to focus on the actual data quality issue. So if we standardize and have each of these steps report to the data error manager

1)in a way that a human can check if it's involved

2)In the way that we can do all fixes or

3)in a way that we can report back to someone at the source end who knows how and give them an actionable piece of intelligence to correct.

 

 

12.Data about your “Bad Data”

 

Poor data quality is going to seep into our systems most frequently when our data is in motion. The ways to design pipelines to capture that bad data and ideally to automate the remediation.  

 

 

 

So capturing and collecting data about your bad data can have important business value. It can allow you to identify flawed processes or training issues close to the source.

 

Maybe you've got one part of your business that is using a misconfigured piece of infrastructure collecting bad data. Collecting the data about your bad data might lead you to discover that in a more timely fashion. You can also uncover and reconcile differences in the way your data is being interpreted.

 

To identify and standardize business definitions, if you've got different teams that might be thinking of statuses for a particular type of entity, those definitions might surface as quote unquote poor data quality. So designing a data pipeline to handle bad data not only improves the immediate data quality in your systems but it can also provide unique and valuable insight into your underlying business processes. Surfacing issues that you might not have otherwise been aware of.

 

 

 

You really need to put risk management in context with this price. What is poor data quality going to cost you if you're a large organization? Poor data into a system can result in you know lost revenue or fail to do correct billing. And there's a quite easy way to assign a monetary value to the risk of not doing that. So it becomes a value judgment assessing the the cost of having that the cost of the risk of having that bad data versus the cost of doing the implementation.

14.Cleaning (preparing) your data

 

Cleaning process goes through our data and gets rid of bad data

 

1)Valid bad data - hard stuff data itself is kind of crappy but there was no issue in how we process the data

2)Invalid bad data - issue with the ETL process you need to go back figure out where something went wrong and you need to fix

 

The reality is, much of your time as a data scientist will spent preparing and “cleaning” your data

⚫Outliers (Valid bad data) - looking at web log data and you see one session ID that keeps coming back over and over and over again it keeps doing something at a ridiculous rate 

⚫Missing data (Valid bad data) - what do you do when data is just not there 

⚫Malicious data - people trying to cheat the system and you don't want those people getting away

⚫Erroneous data - a software bug somewhere in some systems that's just writing out the wrong values 

⚫Irrelevant data (Valid bad data) - not related data

⚫Inconsistent Data - addresses people can write the same address in many different ways 

⚫Formatting -  dates in the some region we always do month/day/year but in other countries they might do day/month/year

 

 

 

15.Data Governance

 

If you were able to monetize your data by understanding what it is and helping your organization, that's the importance of data governance.  That's the value that it brings to your organization.  The one thing I wish I could do was automated that process.

 

 

⚫Discovery - First thing I had to do was understand what I had.  Discover all of the different items. Discovery is a process of understanding all of the different data assets you have across your repositories, which may be in the cloud, on prem, or even from some SAS (Statistical Analysis System) applications.  The hard part is to discovering the data that you don't know about.

⚫Classification - The process of classification is assigning data to different categories. Whether it's customer data, product data, financial data. And providing that label or that classification to it.

⚫Policy - set forth some policies about what we were keeping, what we were donating, and what we were just throwing in the garbage. Policies are about setting guidelines and standards about what to do with your data.

nPersonally identifiable data - Personally identifiable data such as a Social Security number, must be masked.

⚫Rules - Rules are ways to help you to enforce your data policies.

⚫Metadata (labeling) - Metadata, in the data world, helps describe what that data asset is. Simply put, it's like a card catalog at a library that describes a book. It provides the author the subject, the copyright. It tells you where the data came from, what it is, so that when you want to use it, it's easier to find and easier to use when the time comes.

 

16.Poor Data Governance

 

And how many times have you called in to your customer service agent, giving them all your information like your name, date of birth,  account number, and then once you tell them your actual problem, they transfer you to another agent, who then ask you the same exact questions.  Can you give me your name? Date of birth? Acount number? Like if that first conversation had never really happened? this is an example of poor data governance.

It all starts with a central repository for data.  And this central repository has all different types of data like non-confidential and confidential.  Data governance is about protecting confidential data.

 

Data governance, it's about allowing the sharing of this information from that central repository to different departments without exposing important information from you as a customer. And one of the ways that we can do this in an automated fashion is through a data governance framework. 

 

This framework has three main components. 

1)Policy

a)GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

2)Rules to implement this policy. 

a)Data protection rules

b)Governance rules - description on how to handling data

3)Classification of these rules. 

a)business terms - like a unit to describe how to measure the data like by day by month. 

b)Data classes - metadata tells you a summary of what's inside of a data source. 

 

 

With the right policy, rules, and classification in place, I have the base of my framework that can be automated through reference data (Code). 

 

 

17.Data Fabric

 

A data fabric is an architecture and set of data services that provide consistent capabilities across a choice of endpoints spanning hybrid multicloud environments. It is a powerful architecture that standardizes data management practices and practicalities across cloud, on premises, and edge devices.
