*** Describe a situation when you had to solve a difficult problem

 

 

While working at BOCI-PRU, I received a support request from a user who reported that a client was having issues submitting a fund switching request through the platform.

 

I investigate the issue and discover that there's a bug in the Web API code that's causing the problem. Specifically, the API endpoint responsible for processing fund switching requests is not correctly parsing the request payload (Unicode issue), causing it to reject the request and return an error message.

 

To fix the issue, I develop a new version of the Web API endpoint that correctly parses the request payload and ensures that the fund switching request is processed correctly. I thoroughly test the new endpoint on a development server to ensure that it works correctly and can handle all expected edge cases.

 

Before deploying the fix to the live server, I follow the company's processes for making changes to the platform, which require all changes to be reviewed and approved by a formal change management process. I submit a request for approval to the system owner and explain the issue, the solution I have identified, and the testing I have done to ensure that the changes won't cause any unintended side effects.

 

The user acknowledges your request and works with the change management committee to review the proposed changes. I document the proposed changes and the testing that I have done, and approve the changes for deployment.

As a Senior Developer, how to perform system design?

 

Performing system design as a Senior Developer involves the following steps:

 

1. Understand the requirements: Start by understanding the requirements of the system I am designing. Meet with stakeholders, such as business analysts, project managers, and clients, to identify the objectives, user needs, and any constraints or limitations that may impact the design.

 

2. Identify the components: Identify the components or modules of the system, including any hardware, software, databases, and interfaces that are required. Break down the system into smaller, manageable parts that can be designed and developed separately.

 

3. Define the architecture: Choose an appropriate architecture that meets the system requirements, such as a client-server, web-based, or cloud-based architecture. Determine how the components will interact with each other and how data will flow between them.

 

4. Create the data model: Create a data model that defines the structure and relationships of the data that will be used in the system. This includes defining tables, fields, and relationships, as well as any constraints, such as keys or indexes.

 

5. Define the user interface: Design the user interface of the system, including the layout, navigation, and controls. Ensure that the interface is user-friendly and meets the needs of the target users.

 

6. Plan for scalability and performance: Plan for the future scalability and performance of the system by considering factors such as the expected usage volume, data growth, and any potential performance bottlenecks.

 

7. Document the design: Document the system design, including the architecture, data model, interface design, and scalability plan. This documentation can be used by developers, testers, and other stakeholders to ensure that the system is built according to the design.

 

8. Review and iterate: Review the system design with stakeholders and team members to ensure that it meets the requirements and is feasible to implement. Iterate on the design as needed until it meets the needs of the system and stakeholders.

 

Performing system design is an iterative process that involves collaboration with stakeholders, other team members, and subject matter experts. As a Senior Developer, my role is to ensure that the system is designed to meet the requirements, is feasible to implement, and is scalable and performant.

Rapid Application Development (RAD) vs Agile methodologies:

 

1. Approach to Requirements: RAD focuses on quickly gathering and incorporating user requirements through techniques such as prototyping, while Agile methodologies use user stories, backlog, and regular customer feedback for requirements gathering and prioritization.

 

2. Development Speed: RAD is known for its rapid development speed, aiming to deliver a working software product quickly. Agile methodologies also emphasize quick iterations, but the timeframe is typically defined by sprints (e.g., 1-4 weeks).

 

3. Flexibility vs. Structure: RAD allows for more flexibility in changing requirements during development, while Agile methodologies provide a structured framework with predefined practices, roles, and ceremonies, such as daily stand-ups, sprint reviews, and retrospectives.

 

4. Team Composition: RAD often involves a cross-functional team with members from different disciplines working closely together, including end-users and developers. Agile methodologies also emphasize cross-functional teams, but roles and responsibilities are typically more clearly defined, such as Scrum Master, Product Owner, and Development Team.

 

5. Documentation: RAD may have less emphasis on documentation compared to Agile methodologies, as it focuses on rapid development and prototyping. Agile methodologies, on the other hand, typically include documentation such as user stories, sprint backlogs, and sprint reviews.

 

6. Customer Involvement: RAD places a strong emphasis on customer involvement throughout the development process, while Agile methodologies also prioritize regular customer feedback through mechanisms such as sprint reviews and demos.

 

7. Project Size: RAD is often suited for smaller projects or projects with rapidly changing requirements, while Agile methodologies are applicable to a wide range of project sizes and complexities.

 

It's important to note that RAD and Agile are not mutually exclusive, and elements of RAD can be incorporated into Agile methodologies or vice versa based on project requirements and team dynamics. The choice between RAD and Agile depends on factors such as project scope, team size, customer involvement, development speed, and flexibility requirements.

System development methodology

 

System development methodology refers to the process of designing, building, and implementing an information system. The methodology can vary depending on the project's scope, size, and complexity, as well as the organization's culture and goals. Some common system development methodologies include:

 

Waterfall: The waterfall methodology is a linear, sequential approach that follows a specific set of steps in a pre-determined order. The phases include requirements gathering, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance.

 

Agile: The agile methodology is an iterative, flexible approach that prioritizes customer satisfaction and continuous improvement. It involves breaking the project down into smaller, more manageable tasks and delivering them in short cycles called sprints.

 

Rapid Application Development (RAD): The RAD methodology focuses on prototyping and rapid iterations. It involves developing a working model of the system quickly and iterating on it based on user feedback.

 

Scrum: Scrum is a type of agile methodology that focuses on teamwork and collaboration. It involves breaking the project down into short sprints, and each sprint ends with a review and planning session.

 

DevOps: DevOps is a methodology that emphasizes collaboration and communication between development and operations teams. It involves using automation and continuous integration and deployment to streamline the development process.

 

Lean: The lean methodology aims to eliminate waste and increase efficiency in the development process. It involves continuously improving processes, reducing work in progress, and focusing on delivering value to the customer.

 

Each methodology has its strengths and weaknesses, and choosing the right one for a specific project depends on various factors, including the project's goals, timeline, budget, and team's skills and preferences.

 

System development approach

 

A system development approach is a framework or set of principles that guide the process of building an information system. Some of the common system development approaches are:

 

Structured Systems Analysis and Design (SSAD): SSAD is a traditional approach that follows a phased approach to system development. The phases include planning, analysis, design, implementation, and maintenance. The approach focuses on modeling and documentation of the system requirements, and the design is based on logical and physical modeling.

 

Object-Oriented Analysis and Design (OOAD): OOAD is an approach that focuses on modeling the system as a collection of objects that have attributes and behaviors. The approach emphasizes modular design, reusability, and maintainability. OOAD typically involves the use of UML diagrams to model the system's requirements, design, and implementation.

 

Agile Development: Agile development is an approach that emphasizes flexibility and adaptability. The approach involves iterative development, collaboration between the development team and the client, and continuous delivery of working software. Agile development typically involves the use of user stories, sprints, and scrums.

 

Rapid Application Development (RAD): RAD is an approach that aims to accelerate the development process by emphasizing prototyping and iterative development. The approach focuses on user involvement and feedback to refine the system's design and functionality.

 

DevOps: DevOps is an approach that emphasizes the integration of development and operations processes. The approach involves using automation to streamline the development process, continuous integration and delivery, and collaboration between the development and operations teams.

 

Lean: Lean development is an approach that aims to reduce waste and increase efficiency in the development process. The approach involves eliminating non-value-added activities, improving quality, and focusing on customer satisfaction.

 

Choosing the right system development approach depends on various factors, including the project's complexity, the team's skills and expertise, the organization's culture, and the project's goals and requirements.
